Let me ask you something uncomfortable.

If a complete stranger walked up to you today and said, "Give me your money. I'll teach you something valuable in return" — would you do it?

Probably not.

Now what if that same stranger walked in with your most respected colleague, who turned around and said, "I vouch for this person" — would your answer change?

Almost certainly yes.

That's the entire principle behind this post. And it's the thing that changed everything for us when we were just getting started.

## The Problem: You Need Trust to Get Customers, But Customers to Get Trust

When you're starting out, you're dealing with an invisible wall.

You need customers to build credibility. But you need credibility to get customers.

Most first-time founders try to break this loop by talking louder — more content, more claims, more "here's why we're qualified" posts. But that approach almost never works, because the problem isn't visibility.

The problem is trust.

Customers don't buy products. They buy confidence. Confidence that their money won't be wasted. Confidence that the people on the other side know what they're doing. Confidence that choosing you won't be a decision they regret.

When you don't have a track record, that confidence has to come from somewhere else.

## The Solution: Borrow Credibility Before You Own It

When we launched our education business, our only credibility was corporate work experience. Useful — but not enough. Thousands of people have corporate experience. It didn't make students feel safe handing us their time and money.

So instead of trying to manufacture credibility from scratch, we borrowed it.

The first move was simple: we invited professionals from well-known tech companies to conduct sessions for our students. And something shifted immediately.

Before: "Some random platform trying to teach us things."

After: "A platform where professionals from top companies actually come to teach."

Same content. Same team. Completely different perception.

This is how you build trust with customers before your brand name means anything — not by claiming you're trustworthy, but by associating yourself with people your audience already trusts.

## Why It Works: The Psychology Behind Borrowed Credibility

Every time someone encounters a new brand, their brain runs a quick calculation: "If I give these people my time or money, how likely am I to regret it?"

With established brands, the answer comes from track record. With a first-time founder, there is no track record. So the brain defaults to high-risk, low-trust.

When a senior professional from a well-known company agrees to show up on your platform, here's what your audience processes:

> "That person has a reputation to protect. They wouldn't show up for something untrustworthy. This platform must be legitimate."

The respected professional becomes a proxy validator — and their credibility transfers directly to you. This is the Association Heuristic: we judge things by what they're connected to.

There's also a well-documented bias called the Halo Effect at work here. When we have a positive impression of one thing, it spills into everything around it. The teaching didn't change. The frame around it did — and that changed everything.

## How to Apply This Starting This Week

You don't need a big budget. You don't need a famous name. You need to answer one question honestly:

**Who in my space already has the trust of my target audience — and how can I create a genuine reason for us to collaborate?**

It could be a guest session. A joint post. A podcast conversation. A co-hosted event.

The format doesn't matter as much as the signal it sends: someone credible chose to show up here.

Start there. One association, executed well, can do more for your brand than six months of solo content. Because in the beginning, you don't build credibility alone — you build a network of credibility around you.

And over time, the credibility you borrow becomes credibility you own.
