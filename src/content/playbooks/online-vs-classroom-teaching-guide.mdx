Archimedes once said: *"Give me a place to stand, and I shall move the Earth."*

It sounds dramatic at first. Almost arrogant. But read it again — he was not talking about strength. He was talking about **leverage**. He understood that you do not need to be the most powerful force in the room. You need the right position, the right system, and a long enough lever.

That idea applies directly to teaching online vs classroom. Both models require real knowledge and real effort. But only one of them scales.

## The Classroom Has a Hard Ceiling

A teacher inside a physical classroom can reach 30 to 50 students per session. That number is constrained by space, time, and physical presence. The lesson ends when the class ends. Income stops when you stop showing up.

This is not a criticism of classroom teaching. It is a description of its structure. In-person teaching builds deep presence, immediate feedback, and human connection. Those things matter enormously. But the model has a built-in ceiling, and no amount of extra effort breaks through it.

More hours. More sessions. More energy. You push harder, and the results grow — but only in proportion to how much you push. That is effort-based growth. And effort has a limit.

## Online Teaching Changes the Position, Not the Knowledge

The same teacher, the same curriculum, the same expertise — placed behind a camera with an internet connection — can now reach 50,000 students instead of 50. The knowledge has not changed. The leverage has.

This is the core difference when comparing teaching online vs classroom. Online teaching decouples your income from your time. A course recorded once can be purchased at 2am by someone in a different timezone. A video explanation you filmed last year keeps answering questions you would otherwise repeat daily.

Naval Ravikant describes this kind of leverage as the foundation of modern wealth creation — where code, media, and systems work without asking permission. Online teaching is exactly that: media and systems working on your behalf, around the clock, without your direct presence.

The effort goes in once. The value compounds over time.

## What You Actually Give Up and What You Gain

It would be dishonest to frame this as purely one-sided. Online teaching comes with its own real trade-offs.

You lose the immediate read of a room. You lose the ability to adjust in real time based on body language and energy. You lose the natural accountability that comes from students knowing they will see you again on Tuesday. Building trust with an online audience takes longer and requires more deliberate design — through community, feedback loops, and consistent communication.

What you gain, however, is asymmetric. You gain geographic reach. You gain time freedom. You gain the ability to serve students who would never have access to your classroom. You gain a product that can grow without requiring more of your hours.

The question is not which model is better in the abstract. The question is: what are you optimizing for?

## Building a Lever, Not Just Completing Tasks

Most educators who move online still operate with a classroom mindset. They trade one-on-one consulting for one-to-many live sessions, but the logic stays the same — income stops when they stop.

The real shift happens when you stop asking *"how can I work harder today?"* and start asking *"what can I build once that keeps working for me?"*

A recorded curriculum that serves students without your daily presence. A product ecosystem rather than one-time service engagements. A brand that explains your value without you having to repeat it. These are not shortcuts — they are longer levers.

The internet is, by any measure, the most powerful distribution tool teachers have ever had access to. A single course can serve thousands of students simultaneously. A single idea, explained well, can reach people you will never meet. That reach was never available inside four classroom walls.

The choice between teaching online vs classroom is ultimately a question of leverage. Both require skill, preparation, and genuine care for your students. But only one of them can grow while you sleep.

Build the lever. Then choose where to stand.
